Echocardiography FAQ & Clinical Guidance

Should I use LA:Ao or LAD to assess left atrial size?

The LA:Ao ratio has traditionally been the standard, but it can be challenging to measure reliably. Recent recommendations favor the Left Atrial Diameter (LAD) in the long axis. This can be normalized using the allometric scaling formula: LAD (cm) / Weight (kg)0.309. A value > 1.6 is typically considered indicative of left atrial enlargement.

How is LVIDdn calculated in the Echo Calculator?

LVIDdn (normalized Left Ventricular Internal Diameter in diastole) uses the Cornell allometric scaling formula: LVIDd (cm) / Weight (kg)0.294. This allows for the comparison of heart size across different body weights.

What are the EPIC trial criteria for Stage B2 MMVD?

The EPIC trial defines Stage B2 as a patient with a murmur grade ≥ 3/6 and echocardiographic evidence of cardiomegaly, specifically an LA:Ao ratio ≥ 1.6 and an LVIDdn ≥ 1.7.

Should I use breed-specific normals or Cornell scaling?

Standard Cornell scaling is appropriate for most breeds. However, for 'deviant' breeds like Sighthounds or Boxers, published breed-specific reference intervals should be used to avoid mis-staging heart disease.

What parameters are required to calculate the Canine MINE score?

The 4-parameter MINE 1 scoring system requires the LA:Ao ratio, LVIDdn, Fractional Shortening (FS), and E-wave peak velocity. The simplified 3-parameter MINE 2 system utilizes LA:Ao, LVIDdn, and E-wave velocity to classify myxomatous mitral valve disease (MMVD) severity and estimate median survival time (MST). Enter those values in our calculator to get the score calculated instantly and displayed in a printable report.

How is the Qp:Qs ratio calculated on a veterinary echocardiogram?

The Qp:Qs shunt ratio evaluates pulmonary to systemic blood flow. Systemic stroke volume (Qs) is calculated using the LVOT diameter and LVOT VTI, while pulmonary stroke volume (Qp) uses the RVOT diameter and RVOT VTI via the formula: Stroke Volume = VTI × π × (Diameter / 20)^2. A ratio of 1.0 is normal, while values > 1.5 indicate a hemodynamically significant left-to-right shunt.
